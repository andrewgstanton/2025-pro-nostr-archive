At a certain point, you stop being able to stomach the spin.  
You can hear it in corporate language, religious branding, even in your own excuses — the subtle inflation of words that no longer point to anything real. It’s not that you become cynical; it’s that your inner calibration finally starts rejecting noise.

This is what spiritual and moral growth actually feels like: an **allergy to bullshit.**  
The same way the body reacts to toxins, the soul starts reacting to falsity. When you’ve spent enough time seeking what’s true — in money, in faith, in relationships — you begin to feel dishonesty physically, like static in the air.

The challenge is to keep that sensitivity from hardening into contempt. The goal isn’t to see through everything; it’s to see *into* things, until only what’s genuine remains. Anger burns fast; clarity endures.

That’s the work: to cultivate truth without cruelty, honesty without arrogance.  
You can detest hypocrisy and still love the people lost inside it. After all, you were once fluent in the same language. You just decided to stop speaking it.

Integrity isn’t moral superiority; it’s alignment.  
It’s the quiet refusal to perform when reality is enough.  
And once you’ve tasted that freedom, illusion feels like poison.
