Airports are the temples of bureaucracy. Every traveler passes through the same rituals: remove your shoes, unzip your bag, show your papers. The system isn’t built on trust; it’s built on suspicion. You are guilty until scanned, verified, and waved through.  

But what if identity itself were portable — cryptographically provable, impossible to forge, yet private to you? A world where your proof of integrity travels *with* you, not *against* you. That’s the promise of sovereign identity: replacing suspicion with verification, fear with mathematics.  

The current model is reactive. It checks for contraband, not character. Its cost is enormous — time, manpower, humiliation. Every checkpoint repeats what the previous one already confirmed: you are who you say you are. In a decentralized model, that redundancy vanishes. A single signature could replace a dozen checkpoints.  

The implications stretch far beyond airports. Every border, loan office, or election booth could one day operate on this principle: verify truth, not allegiance. Nostr already does this for speech. Extend it to movement, and you have a trust fabric for civilization.  

Skeptics will say: but what about the bad actors? The same question was asked of open networks, and the answer was always the same — bad actors exist *because* of opacity, not openness. Transparency disarms them.  

The irony is that true safety feels calmer, not harsher. A decentralized gate would process more people, more honestly, with fewer hands in their pockets. We’d rediscover dignity in transit. The world would feel smaller again — not because technology shrank it, but because trust expanded.  

The lines will never vanish. But the reason for standing in them could.
