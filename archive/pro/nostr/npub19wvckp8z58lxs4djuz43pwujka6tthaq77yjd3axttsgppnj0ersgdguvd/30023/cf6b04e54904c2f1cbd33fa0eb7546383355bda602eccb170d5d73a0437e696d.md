Andrew G. Stanton - Oct. 21, 2025

People often say Bitcoin is “too volatile,” as though volatility were a moral flaw. They treat calm prices as virtuous and wild charts as a sign of danger. But stability, when manufactured, is the most deceptive calm of all.

Look around: every market swings. Stocks whipsaw on quarterly earnings and central-bank whispers. Bonds collapse when the winds of interest rates shift. Currencies devalue overnight on policy reversals or geopolitical tremors. Even the so-called “safe havens” are not immune — gold languishes for years, then surges unpredictably when fear returns. The world is volatile because humans are volatile.

Yet Bitcoin’s volatility feels different because it exposes something most systems try to hide. Traditional markets externalize their uncertainty; they push it into accounting tricks, monetary manipulation, and institutional smoothing. The goal is to make us believe the world can be managed. Bitcoin refuses that illusion. It doesn’t suppress the chaos — it lets it breathe.

Volatility, in this light, becomes a kind of honesty. The chart is not a threat but a confession: here is what discovery looks like when no one is in charge. Each candle, each oscillation, is the human psyche learning what ungoverned truth feels like.

We confuse comfort with safety. Fiat systems lull us into calm by muting reality. Bitcoin confronts us with the raw noise of freedom. It’s like moving from a padded room into open air — at first the wind stings your skin, but eventually you realize that’s what real weather feels like.

What if volatility isn’t the enemy but the evidence that something real is happening? After all, creation itself began in turbulence — the void, the deep waters, the Spirit hovering and dividing light from darkness. Order arose not from stillness but from motion. Likewise, Bitcoin’s volatility is not disorder but genesis — the continual process of price discovering truth.

When we say “Bitcoin is volatile,” we are describing humanity’s inability to price incorruptible value. We are watching the market wrestle with the meaning of absolute scarcity in a world of endless printing. That’s not chaos; that’s revelation.

Theologians sometimes speak of sanctification — the lifelong process of aligning our inner world with divine reality. Bitcoin’s price history looks like sanctification in graph form: sharp corrections, renewed highs, repeated tests. Each crash is a kind of repentance — the market purging its illusions. Each rally, a new measure of faith.

Volatility is not what defines Bitcoin. Transparency is. The volatility is simply what transparency looks like when it collides with human greed, fear, and misunderstanding.

The fiat world paints its stability with borrowed colors — cheap credit, hidden inflation, “soft landings” that never last. Its stillness is anesthetic, not peace. Bitcoin’s motion, by contrast, is the restlessness of something alive — a heartbeat, not a seizure.

When you look at a Bitcoin chart, you are not seeing chaos; you are seeing a living organism learning to breathe in a toxic atmosphere. Each spike of volatility is a gasp of truth trying to find equilibrium.

Maybe, then, the point isn’t to tame the volatility, but to understand what it’s telling us. The waves are not against us; they are revealing the contours of the ocean floor — the structure of the unseen world beneath.

Bitcoin’s volatility reminds us that we are participants in discovery, not spectators of control. And if the waves still scare us, perhaps it’s not Bitcoin that needs to change — perhaps it’s us.

The point isn’t to find peace by making the water still. It’s to learn how to float.
