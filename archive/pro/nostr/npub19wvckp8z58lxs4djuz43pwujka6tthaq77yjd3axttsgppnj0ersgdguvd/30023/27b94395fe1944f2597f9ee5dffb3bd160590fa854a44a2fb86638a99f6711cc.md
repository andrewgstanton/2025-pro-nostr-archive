Andrew G. Stanton - Oct. 11, 2025

Every builder learns about leverage the hard way.
Sometimes you borrow money; sometimes you borrow time, attention, or trust. The results can look the same for a while—growth, expansion, speed—but underneath, the source of your leverage determines whether it will hold or break.

I’ve felt both.
The first kind of leverage—debt-based—gives power up front and demands payment later. It’s the leverage of the impatient. You use tomorrow’s strength today. It feels efficient until it turns cruel. Markets crash, lenders call, energy runs out. The bill always comes due.

The second kind—principle-based—moves slower but compounds longer. It’s alignment leverage. You plant roots in something incorruptible and let time do the multiplication. This is the leverage of truth: integrity, patience, proof-of-work. It doesn’t swing as wide, but it endures.

When I first started trading, I thought leverage was just numbers—a ratio on a chart. But I see now it’s spiritual as much as financial. Every shortcut carries an unspoken faith in the future: I’ll be able to cover this later. Sometimes you can; often you can’t. Freedom begins when you stop mortgaging peace of mind for velocity.

Debt leverage always promises control, but it feeds on fear. You end up serving the tools meant to serve you. The interest clock ticks whether you sleep or not. Your emotional balance sheet shrinks, even when the portfolio grows. The invisible cost is presence—the ability to live in real time instead of calculating future risk.

Principle leverage feels less exciting. There’s no instant feedback loop, no dopamine chart. It’s the long obedience of building systems that outlast moods. It’s saving when no one’s watching, writing when no one’s reading, coding when no one’s paying yet. You earn returns in trust before you earn them in currency.

Bitcoin taught me that kind of leverage.
Proof-of-work is patience materialized. It’s time secured by honesty. No one gets to borrow hashpower from the future. The network grows because each block confirms the last. That’s alignment leverage in pure form—energy spent once, truth preserved forever.

I don’t always apply this as consistently as I’d like.
I still catch myself borrowing against peace—stretching a day too far, saying yes to too many things, convincing myself I can carry it all. Even knowing what’s right doesn’t mean I live it perfectly. But noticing the gap between what I know and what I practice is part of the work too. It’s how character compounds.

Entrepreneurs talk about “other people’s money” as the holy grail. But the most powerful form of leverage is other people’s trust—and that one can’t be faked or forced. It’s earned through consistent proof-of-work across time. Betray it once and the cost is exponential.

When I look back at the moments I felt most trapped, the common thread wasn’t circumstance; it was borrowed conviction. I let others’ urgency define my pace. That’s emotional leverage—the subtlest and most dangerous kind. You owe interest in anxiety. The only remedy is sovereignty: owning your own timeline again.

In Continuum, I try to design tools that embody principle leverage—small scripts that do honest work, identities that verify themselves, systems that don’t rely on endless maintenance. The goal isn’t speed but sustainability. Builders forget this: sustainability is profit, just measured over longer horizons.

The beauty of alignment leverage is that it scales with rest. It keeps compounding even when you’re not pushing. Like Bitcoin’s difficulty adjustment, it adapts to maintain equilibrium. You can walk away for a day and not lose ground, because truth doesn’t need babysitting.

In faith, it’s the same. Grace is not a loan; it’s a current. You don’t repay it; you flow with it. The more you align with it, the stronger it carries you. That’s holy leverage—power multiplied by surrender.

If I could tell my younger self one thing, it would be this:
Never trade slow freedom for fast bondage.
Don’t borrow against peace.
Invest in rhythms that renew themselves.

True leverage isn’t borrowed power; it’s aligned purpose.
And purpose—when rooted in truth—doesn’t collapse under pressure.
It multiplies, quietly, forever.
