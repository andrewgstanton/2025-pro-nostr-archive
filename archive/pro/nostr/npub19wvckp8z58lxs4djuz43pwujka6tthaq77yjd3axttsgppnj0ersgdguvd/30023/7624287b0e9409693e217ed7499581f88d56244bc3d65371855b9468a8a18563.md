Andrew G. Stanton - Oct. 9, 2025

## 1. The Fear That Calls Itself “Good News”

Scroll through YouTube or Christian LinkedIn and you’ll hear the same refrain:  
*“If you don’t believe X, you’re in danger of hellfire.”*  
*“God’s justice demands satisfaction.”*  
*“Jesus took the punishment you deserved.”*

These phrases sound familiar because they have dominated Protestant preaching for centuries. They make for powerful altar calls and viral sound bites. But what happens when we step back and ask the most basic question: **what kind of God do they describe?**

If the story is that God the Father killed His beloved Son so that He could forgive the rest of us—creatures He otherwise “hated”—then the cross becomes divine child sacrifice. The gospel turns into a threat. And love, the very thing it claims to proclaim, becomes conditional and fragile.

Many believers quietly feel this tension. They sing of “amazing grace,” yet somewhere underneath lurks anxiety: *what if grace runs out? what if I believed the wrong way?* The fear never really leaves because the foundation of the message is fear itself.

---

## 2. Where That Story Came From

The idea that God’s justice required a literal payment of punishment arose gradually.  
In the 11th century, **Anselm of Canterbury** framed sin as an offense against divine honor that had to be satisfied.  
By the 16th, **John Calvin** reshaped that into legal categories—penal substitution.  
Sin was now a crime, the cross a courtroom, and Jesus the substitute prisoner executed so that the rest could go free.

This model had rhetorical force during eras when kings and judges embodied authority. But it was never the only, or even the earliest, Christian explanation of the cross. The **apostolic and patristic writers** spoke of something richer: *Christus Victor*—the victory of God’s love over the powers of death, sin, and corruption.  
Athanasius, Irenaeus, and Gregory of Nyssa all saw the cross as a rescue mission, not a penal transaction.

When Western theology lost that cosmic horizon, the gospel shrank to a personal legal pardon. Justice eclipsed mercy, and fear replaced wonder.

---

## 3. The Biblical Story That Refuses to Fit the Courtroom

Read the New Testament without importing centuries of commentary and a different pattern emerges.

- **Mark 10:45** — “The Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to give his life as a *ransom* for many.”  
  A ransom frees captives; it isn’t a payment to placate the captor.

- **1 Timothy 2:6** — “Who gave himself as a ransom for all.”  
  The direction is outward, toward liberation.

- **Colossians 2:15** — “He disarmed the rulers and authorities and made a public spectacle of them, triumphing over them by the cross.”  
  That’s warfare language: Christ defeats evil; He doesn’t satisfy it.

- **2 Corinthians 5:19** — “God was *in Christ* reconciling the world to Himself.”  
  The Father and the Son are not opposing parties; they are one will acting in love.

Everywhere the movement is **reconciliation**, not retaliation. The cross reveals what God has always been like—it does not change Him.

---

## 4. The Human Cost of the Hellfire Gospel

When fear becomes the engine of faith, the fruit is predictable.

- **Psychological captivity.** Believers live on probation, measuring devotion by anxiety rather than peace.  
- **Moral paralysis.** Obedience becomes avoidance of punishment, not participation in love.  
- **Public witness distortion.** Outsiders hear threats instead of hope and conclude, rightly, that a God who needs blood to forgive cannot be good.

Whole generations have walked away from Christianity not because they rejected Jesus, but because they couldn’t stomach a Father who tortures His children forever.  

If eternal conscious torment is the frame, love is never quite believable.

---

## 5. The Victorious Gospel

The **ransom** or **Christus Victor** view doesn’t deny justice; it redefines it.  
Justice is not retribution but restoration—the setting right of a cosmos enslaved by death.  
In this vision:

- The Father and Son act together to rescue humanity.  
- The cross exposes and defeats the powers of violence, shame, and accusation.  
- Resurrection is not a postscript; it’s the proof that death’s reign is broken.  

Hell, in this light, is not an eternal torture chamber but the ongoing ruin of resisting love—the self-chosen distance that healing refuses to force. Love wins, not by coercion, but by outlasting everything that is not love.

This is not modern sentimentalism; it is the logic of Scripture itself. *“For God so loved the world that He gave His only Son…”*—not to change His mind about us, but to change our minds about Him.

---

## 6. Why It Matters Now

Online Christianity is drifting toward spectacle and outrage. Algorithms reward fear.  
“Hellfire” thumbnails get clicks; nuanced theology does not. Yet if we who claim Christ continue to preach fear, we reinforce the same manipulative dynamics that the gospel was meant to end.

The world doesn’t need louder threats. It needs a clearer picture of God’s heart.  
When the church recovers the victorious, restorative vision of the cross, it regains moral credibility. It stops using terror as marketing and starts embodying hope.

For communities exploring digital sovereignty or Bitcoin or Nostr—spaces already allergic to control—this shift matters doubly. A coercive gospel will never persuade people who have tasted freedom. Only a message that mirrors true liberation can.

---

## 7. A Call to Teachers and Thinkers

If you preach, write, or teach, test this question against every sermon and post:

> “Does my message make God look more like Jesus or less?”

If it makes Him look less—more vindictive, more transactional, more fragile—then something is off.  
The early church grew not by threatening hell but by embodying a love stronger than death.

Revisiting atonement isn’t heresy; it’s repentance for having believed too little in God’s goodness.

---

## 8. Conclusion: The Fire That Heals

There *is* fire in the gospel—but it’s the fire of purification, not torture; the flame of love that consumes dross, not souls.  
God’s judgment is His mercy seen from the other side.

The good news is still good: the Father did not need to be convinced to forgive.  
He has always been as loving as Jesus showed Him to be.  
The cross is not divine violence; it is divine vulnerability.  
It is God entering our hell to empty it from within.

That is the victory worth proclaiming.
